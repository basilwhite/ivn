154: What is an IVN XE "IVN" XE "IVN" ?
155: Program management data is a strategic asset, because it serves as a basis of evidence to modernize program and project data systems and make more evidence-based decisions.
156: Program managers perform comprehensive research of existing requirements to support new requirements. More exhaustive searches reduce the risk of creating new requirements that contradict with existing requirements. Asserting to leadership that a policymaking team has successfully identified a full inventory of relevant policy is challenging and uncertain.
157: Comparing laws, regulations, policies, programs and performance measures for relevance, etc., helps to find the policy "outliers" that would be less visible to most practitioners in the knowledge domain.
158: The Federal Government of the United States governs the Nation using an interdependent network of requirements, including laws, rules, agreements, goals, and metrics, that preserve, protect, and defend the Constitution of the United States and those under its authority. To represent Federal requirements as an interdependent network, we can identify source documents and their requirements as nodes in the network.
159: To observe how delivering requirements adds delivery value to other requirements, we can draw a connector with an arrow from a requirement that provides or “enables” value to another “dependent” requirement receiving value.
160: The Integrated Value Network (IVN XE "IVN" XE "IVN" ) Initiative is an academic and industry project to represent public policy as an interdependent network of requirements. The dataset for this network is a searchable database of public policies and the documents they authorize, connected by how their requirements enable each other to progress toward their end state. Public policies in the IVN include Public Laws, the U.S. Code of Federal Regulations, program directives, Executive Orders and guidance, strategic plans, performance plans and reports, and other content that aligns to the requirements in these sources or derives its authority from them. Requirements in these sources include goals, regulations, program directions, Executive Branch compliance documents, strategic objectives, performance measures, and orders.
161: IVN XE "IVN" Inputs and Outputs
162: Some of these outlying policies represent important but overlooked stakeholders; finding these outliers helps develop programs that are relevant and useful to a broader audience.
163: The IVN XE "IVN" as a Data System
164: Given the highly connected nature of entities in the IVN XE "IVN" , the presence of many-to-many relationships between entities, and the potential of read/write requirements on entities and relationships in the IVN, I think leveraging a graph database makes the most sense. In which case, Azure CosmosDB for Apache Gremlin probably provides a path forward. We would love your thoughts on this. We are open to any data model that will effectively ingest, transform, store, and serve IVN data.

166: This approach to depict and understand public policy requirements as a network helps Federal agencies and other stakeholders understand and predict systematic causes and effects of policy decisions, maximize intended effects of policy change, and mitigate unintended consequences. Research and management of these policy interdependencies also directs continuous learning, improvement, allocation of resources, and adaptation of policymaking operations as defined in the Evidence-Based Policymaking Act of 2018.
167: Three branches of U.S. Federal Government - Executive, Legislative, and Judicial - have their own sets of responsibilities and powers connecting each other across networks of laws, rules, agreements, goals, and metrics.
168: This network of U.S. Government requirements also transacts delivery value with state and local governments connected by their own requirements (e.g., procedures for Federal grant applications).
169: U.S. Government participates in a few international organizations and treaties connected to the Federal Government through yet another network of requirements. These organizations and treaties regulating international trade provide aid to developing nations and promote peace and security.
170: Therefore, U.S. laws, guidance, rules, agreements, goals, and metrics may be understood as a network connected to several other networks. Understanding, communicating, and improving this network in a traditionally siloed environment helps the U.S. Federal Government operate, evolve, and improve for maximum effect. A networked understanding of U.S. Federal Government requirements clarifies how the Government works, finds common ground, and how to improve it based on comprehensive understanding of cause and effect. The IVN XE "IVN" approach offers a powerful base of evidence for creating value and maximizing positive outcomes for Federal agencies and other stakeholders who transact business value across these networks.
171: An IVN XE "IVN" of Federal laws, strategies, regulations, directives, and performance measures provides a systemic map of how they interact to deliver compliance. This map provides optics to manage interactions strategically, scope efforts, and make policy decisions with constructed systemic effects.
172: Strategic goals for the IVN XE "IVN" XE "IVN" Program
173: Establish a centralized database of U.S. Executive Branch policies, strategies, regulations, and performance measures. This database should be accessible to all relevant stakeholders and should include detailed information on each policy, strategy, regulation, and performance measure.
174: Develop a comprehensive framework for integrating various elements of the U.S. Executive Branch policies, strategies, regulations, and performance measures. This framework should include clear definition of purpose for each element as well as how they interact with one another.
175: Create a system for tracking and monitoring implementation of the U.S. Executive Branch policies, strategies, regulations, and performance measures. This system should include a mechanism for collecting data on effectiveness for each element as well as a way to identify areas where improvement is necessary.
176: Develop a set of metrics for measuring success of U.S. Executive Branch policies, strategies, regulations, and performance measures. These metrics should be tailored to specific goals of the U.S. Executive Branch and should be updated regularly to reflect environmental changes.
177: Establish a feedback loop between U.S. Executive Branch and stakeholders ensuring policies, strategies, regulations, and performance measures meet intended objectives. This feedback loop should include regular meetings between stakeholders and the U.S. Executive Branch to discuss progress and identify areas for improvement.
178: Develop a system for evaluating overall effectiveness of U.S. Executive Branch policies, strategies, regulations, and performance measures. This system should include a comprehensive review of data collected from a tracking and monitoring system as well as evaluation of stakeholder feedback.
179: Who creates governance sources XE "governance sources" indexed in an IVN XE "IVN" ?
180: Governance sources are created by executives, lawmakers, policymakers, regulatory bodies, strategic planners, performance researchers, and anyone who creates content to support delivery of the scope of these sources.
181: Who uses IVN XE "IVN" and what goals does IVN help them achieve?
182: Policymakers, planners, and analysts use the IVN XE "IVN" as a basis of evidence for products serving a variety of strategic goals.
183: Help responsible offices of policies, plans, measures, priorities, and portfolios identify and communicate their value to and within Federal Governance Structure.
184: Change policies, plans, measures, and priorities to increase value.
185: Advocate for change to laws, policies, plans, measures, and priorities increasing capability of the Federal Governance Structure to receive value.
186: Produce maps (networks) of initiatives, offices, programs, and outcomes that are logical and data-driven models of portfolios.
187: Convert unstructured data into structured decision-quality information therefore becoming enterprise knowledge.
188: What are the risks that an IVN XE "IVN" would help you mitigate?
189: Risks mitigated by an IVN include: duplication and/or replication of efforts, or contradicting efforts; failure to communicate the effectiveness and efficiency of an organization; handicapping quick response times.
190: What are the currently missed opportunities that an IVN XE "IVN" would help you recognize and leverage?
191: Budget and staffing requests based on IVN XE "IVN" data have a broader alignment to requirements across multiple levels of government. Clients report that most alignments we find from their program to the laws and regs they serve were unknown to the client. When you have the IVN catalog of the laws and regulations your program serves, you have a stronger justification for your budget, authorities and staff. Listing all the programs that serve a regulation or law allows you to measure the overall cost of compliance, and estimate the savings from legislative relief.
192: What products do IVN XE "IVN" users create based on IVN data?
193: People use IVN XE "IVN" data to make derivative products serving as a basis of evidence for reporting and improving value delivery across the Federal Governance Structure.
194: The Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) built a prototype data model to link its governance documents that deliver and receive business value to each other like the President’s Management Agenda, Strategic Plan, Agency Priority Goals, VA Secretary’s Priorities, Department policies, and performance measures. After VA started populating the model, the IVN XE "IVN" team helped VA offices update their strategic communication documents using IVN data on value linkages to and from their documents.
195: VA subsequently discovered that a set of laws, (GPRA, Government Performance and Results Act Modernization Act (GPRAMA), Evidence Act) and OMB policies (A-11, A-123, and M-19-23), when viewed together generate a specific network of mandated documents with specific mandated linkages mentioned in OMB A-123 as Federal Governance Structure. This Structure became the network defining what documents to add to the database and how to link them. We now refer to networks generated by the model as IVNs.
196: FAA developed and used a pilot IVN XE "IVN" to support strategic communications and web project initiatives. The database is consulted to support corporate strategic communications efforts (e.g., communications campaigns) and website initiatives supporting communications to foster collaboration across office lines of business and supporting ability to meet individual office business plans goals and performance measures.
197: GSA’s performance.gov team reviewed the IVN XE "IVN" to inform how to enter, report, and track performance measures. The IVN influenced use of Touchpoints to support data entry of Agency Priority Goals (APGs) - https://github.com/GSA/EDX/blob/main/processes/data-collections.md - and link APGs through Agency Strategic Plans to metrics for traceability and to inform a design for their dashboard reporting system.
198: The IVN XE "IVN" provides a knowledge base to ask and answer questions related to alignment of governance documents across organizations and sources.
199: The IVN XE "IVN" shows how governance documents derive authority from orders, regulation, and law. This provides a visual basis of evidence for policymaking in accordance with Foundations for Evidence-Based Policymaking Act of 2018 (Public Law No: 115-435).
200: The IVN XE "IVN" depicts program organizations as a subnetwork of value they provide to an overall organization and external stakeholders. This mapping of “territory of value” clarifies how to communicate value created by the organization, where to change priorities in the enterprise to increase value, and how to restructure an organization mindful of systemic effects from those changes.
201: Federal Governance Structure XE "Federal Governance Structure" (FGS)
202: From OMB A-123, Management's Responsibility for Enterprise Risk Management and Internal Control: “Federal leaders and managers are responsible for…a governance structure defined through…sources, including laws…. Executive directives and Agency policies.”
203: Source documents directing U.S. Executive branch governance are a complex network of laws, regulations, goals, policies, and performance measures. They link to each other in various ways; sometimes as sets of governance informing a particular program, sometimes as authorities to operate a program, and some relationships are linked by Executive Branch direction or Public Law.
204: Some of these mandated linkages include:
205: President’s Management Agenda to APGs, Agency Performance Measures, and Executive Orders
206: Agency Strategic Plans to President’s Management Agenda, Agency Performance Measures, Agency Learning Agenda, Agency Risk Register, and Agency Priority Goals
207: Evidence Act Capacity Assessment to Agency’s Budget Submission and Learning Agenda
208: Performance Measures to Agency Business Operations and APGs
209: Agency Policies to U.S. Code and Responsible Offices
210: When these linkages are represented as a network a structure emerges.
211: Figure SEQ Figure \* ARABIC1: Federal Governance Structure (FGS)
212: Federal Governance Structure XE "Federal Governance Structure" (FGS) is complex and representing it as a network provides understanding of alignments in both a useful and universal way. Indexing allows for comprehensive program and planning actions resulting in systemic and measurable effects.
213: This network is not a product but rather it is a logical model applying policy and law to basic set theory. Whether policymakers are aware of this network or choose to acknowledge alignments within this system impact Federal service, emergent risks, and influence on and from external stakeholders. Vision for the IVN XE "IVN" Project is that policymakers benefit from understanding this structure to build a common understanding of governance and coordinated actions.
214: Another analogy, Federal Governance Structure functions like a "power grid" structure of strategic "powerplant" documents providing governance value and operational "substation" documents receiving value. Since the network emerged in ad hoc fashion there are underserved substations as well as substations drawing power from powerplants receiving no credit for providing power. This is due to the fact serving a neighborhood not of a part of original powerplant building intent is not foreseen but it is inter-connected (i.e., common ground). Not all insights are a priori.
215: Concluding, Federal agencies and other stakeholders may create and apply an IVN XE "IVN" to navigate complex and interconnected policy challenges and create societal value through evidence-based planning, policymaking, and progress/evolution evaluations.

160: The Integrated Value Network (IVN XE "IVN" XE "IVN" ) Initiative is an academic and industry project to represent public policy as an interdependent network of requirements. The dataset for this network is a searchable database of public policies and the documents they authorize, connected by how their requirements enable each other to progress toward their end state. Public policies in the IVN include Public Laws, the U.S. Code of Federal Regulations, program directives, Executive Orders and guidance, strategic plans, performance plans and reports, and other content that aligns to the requirements in these sources or derives its authority from them. Requirements in these sources include goals, regulations, program directions, Executive Branch compliance documents, strategic objectives, performance measures, and orders.

327: Decompose larger, more complex requirements into smaller, more manageable components until each component with no subordinate components is a single, indivisible requirement that cannot be broken down further into smaller requirements. This feature of requirements is called "atomicity." An atomic requirement represents the most granular and fundamental unit of a requirement necessary for the system or product being developed. This ensures that each requirement is more easily understood, implemented, tracked, measurable, and testable. Decomposing requirements for atomicity helps to ensure that all aspects of a requirement are addressed and that the overall project scope is well-defined.
328: When considering the scope or granularity of sources, consider setting components to the scope that is feasibly adaptable by the governing organization of that source. Keeping the descriptions distinct for each component at each level allows for situations where components can align at a smaller or larger scope in a way that is less reliable at a larger or smaller scale. 406: Common features of enabling sources
407: Because enabling sources have components that add value to, or "enable" dependent components of dependent sources, enabling sources tend to be subordinate to the authority that owns the dependent source. For example, directives that cite U.S. Code regulations are enabling sources of those regulations, and performance measures are enabling sources of the strategic objectives they measure.
408: Enabling sources are often products of the authority requesting analysis products based on IVN XE "IVN" data. This is because all three goals of the IVN (report value to the enterprise, improve value added to the enterprise, increase the capability of the enterprise to receive value) can be achieved when an IVN analysis product focuses on one enabling source. For example, an IVN analysis product that shows alignment from a strategic plan as an enabling source can report how this source adds value to specific components of multiple stakeholders, recommend how to increase this value mindful of these alignments, and recommend how to solicit these stakeholders to change their policies, plans, etc., to receive more value.
409: What are common dependent source features
410: Because dependent sources have components that demand value from, or "depend" on enabling components of enabling sources, dependent sources tend to be superordinate to the authority that owns the enabling source.
411: For example, the PMA is a dependent source, because it "depends" on Agency programs and plans that supply value to "enable" the enterprise to advance toward the end state of one of the components in the PMA.
412: Dependent sources also are more likely to update on an established schedule, such as strategic plans and annual reports.

427: The sources, or governance documents, are decomposed into individually deliverable "components." Analysts map, or “crosswalk”, these components, by viewing two governance documents at a time and finding and documenting the connections where a component of one document adds business value to a component of the other document. Source documents and their components that add business value are called enabling sources, and documents and their components that receive business value are called dependent sources and components. Source documents may not have clearly defined components, or their components may have too large a scope (or too small a scope) to map to dependent sources. In those cases, identifying the correct (feasible) scope of the documents and their components may require additional analysis.

729: Integrated value networking requires an understanding of the concept of scope as defined in the fields of project management, program management, and business analysis. The Project Management Institute (PMI) defines project scope as the work performed to deliver a product, service, or result (capability) with the specified features and functions. For the purposes of IVN XE "IVN" , we adapt this concept of project scope as our definition of value. In other words, the scope of a goal, measure, or regulation, or order is the definition of the end state of that governance document. So, when governance sources and their components add value to each other in a network, it means that delivering the scope of the governing source component delivers some objectively measurable feature of the scope of another governance document.
741: Sources for understanding these concepts include A Guide to the Project Management Body of Knowledge (PMBOK), other PMI references (The Standard for Project Management) that support the PMBOK, and OMB’s Circular No. A-11 (2022). Additional amplifying information for these concepts can be found in the Program Management Improvement Accountability Act (PMIAA) and the Government Performance and Results Act Modernization Act (GPRAMA). Find citations for laws and the OMB guidance for those laws that define program, project, portfolio, and other concepts (legal and enforceable).
742: After this, new analysts need functional definitions of integrated, value, and networks specific to IVN XE "IVN" analysis. Integration equals the connection of distinct elements to support each other or support a common goal or to receive value from a shared source. Also, analysts need to understand the net effect of these connections. Value is the net effect or result of the importance, worth, or usefulness of measurable change. This includes monetary worth, quality of life, or increased information (knowledge), and proof of compliance.
